When to Book with CED vs. Seek Emergency Care

Some eye problems are time-sensitive and can cause permanent sight loss if treatment is delayed. Use this quick guide for awareness only.

Seek emergency care now (nearest hospital / 911)

  • Sudden vision loss in one or both eyes, even if it improves.
  • New dark curtain/shadow over vision, or sudden flashes with many new floaters.
  • Severe eye pain with red eye, nausea, or vomiting.
  • Eye injury, chemical splash, or sudden eye swelling with vision changes.
  • Sudden double vision with severe headache, weakness, or trouble speaking.

Book with Cedarbrae Eye Doctors (same week when possible)

  • Gradual blur, glare, eye strain, or night-driving difficulty.
  • Persistent dry, itchy, watery, or irritated eyes without emergency signs.
  • Contact lens discomfort, recurring redness, or frequent styes.
  • Diabetes eye checks, glaucoma/cataract monitoring, and annual exams.
  • Questions about prevention, seasonal triggers, or OHIP eligibility.

If unsure in Ontario: call 811 or use Health811. Reference info: retinal detachment warning signs and angle-closure glaucoma emergency symptoms.

    January: Dry Eyes

    Cold air and forced-air heating across Scarborough often worsen winter dryness.

    • Check for burning, gritty sensation, fluctuating vision, or reflex tearing.
    • Check home humidity and run a humidifier in bedrooms if air is very dry.
    • Check drop labels and switch to preservative-free tears for frequent use.

    Why book at CED: persistent dry eye can be from meibomian gland dysfunction, medication effects, or eyelid disease that needs targeted treatment.

    February: Indoor Eye Strain

    Indoor work and school screen time peaks in late winter and can drive digital eye strain.

    • Check for headaches, tired eyes, neck strain, and end-of-day blur.
    • Check your setup: screen at arm's length, top of monitor near eye level.
    • Check blink habits with the 20-20-20 rule and frequent full blinks.

    Why book at CED: a glasses or contact lens update plus dry-eye support can reduce daily strain and improve focus comfort.

    March: Early Allergies

    Early spring triggers can begin before full bloom, especially with windy days.

    • Check for itch, redness, tearing, and light sensitivity after outdoor exposure.
    • Check that symptoms are in both eyes; one painful red eye needs urgent assessment.
    • Check pollen timing and keep windows closed on high-count days.

    Why book at CED: CED can distinguish allergy from infection or dry eye and recommend the safest drop plan for your eyes.

    April: Tree Pollen

    Tree pollen in the GTA commonly peaks in spring and can inflame the ocular surface.

    • Check for morning itch and swollen lids after commuting or yard activity.
    • Check lens comfort; consider glasses on high-pollen days.
    • Check post-outdoor routine: shower, eyelid rinse, and clean pillowcases.

    Why book at CED: unmanaged allergy can destabilize contact lens wear and mimic other conditions that need different treatment.

    May: UV Protection

    Longer daylight means more cumulative UV exposure while driving and outdoors.

    • Check sunglasses for UVA/UVB coverage and comfortable wraparound fit.
    • Check your local UV Index before outdoor sports and school pickup hours.
    • Check children and teens have reliable eye sun protection every day.

    Why book at CED: CED can help choose protective lenses and monitor UV-related eye changes over time.

    June: Chlorine and Irritation

    Pool and splash-pad season can trigger irritation, especially for contact lens wearers.

    • Check for redness that lasts more than a few hours after swimming.
    • Check goggle fit and avoid exposing contact lenses to pool or lake water.
    • Check for pain, light sensitivity, or blurred vision after water exposure.

    Why book at CED: water-related irritation can become infection quickly; early care protects vision and comfort.

    July: Summer Dryness and Dehydration

    Heat, wind, and car or office AC can accelerate tear evaporation.

    • Check for dryness during long drives, patio time, and air-conditioned spaces.
    • Check hydration and carry preservative-free lubricating drops when outdoors.
    • Check if vision clears with blinking; that pattern suggests tear-film instability.

    Why book at CED: CED can map dry-eye severity and build a stepwise plan before symptoms become chronic.

    August: Travel and Contact Lens Hygiene

    Late-summer travel often disrupts routine lens care and sleep schedules.

    • Check that you replace lens cases every three months and never top off old solution.
    • Check you have backup glasses in carry-on and beach bags.
    • Check for red-eye warning signs after flights, camping, or poor sleep.

    Why book at CED: if contacts become uncomfortable, an exam can rule out corneal irritation before it escalates.

    September: Back-to-School Screen Time

    School and work routines return, and near-work demand rises sharply.

    • Check for squinting, short attention span in reading, or complaints of board blur.
    • Check homework ergonomics: lighting, screen distance, and posture.
    • Check whether annual family eye exams are up to date in Ontario.

    Why book at CED: early correction supports learning, confidence, and reduced fatigue for students and parents alike.

    October: Fall Allergens and Air Quality

    Ragweed and variable air quality can prolong irritation into mid-fall.

    • Check Toronto AQHI before longer outdoor exercise or commuting.
    • Check home filters and reduce indoor irritants as heating systems restart.
    • Check for burning or watering when AQHI rises or smoke drifts into the area.

    Why book at CED: CED can separate allergy, dryness, and environmental irritation and personalize treatment timing.

    November: Early Darkness and Glare

    Low-angle sun and darker commutes can expose vision quality issues quickly.

    • Check for halos, starbursts, or poor contrast during evening driving.
    • Check glasses coatings and windshield cleanliness to reduce glare scatter.
    • Check diabetes and blood-pressure follow-up if vision fluctuates.

    Why book at CED: new night-vision symptoms can be an early sign of cataract or retinal issues that benefit from prompt review.

    December: Dry Eye and Age Concerns

    Winter dryness plus age-related risks make year-end eye checks especially valuable.

    • Check for reading-light dependence, fading contrast, or slower adaptation at night.
    • Check medication lists for dryness side effects and bring updates to your exam.
    • Check OHIP eligibility by age or medical condition before booking in Ontario.

    Why book at CED: schedule your annual exam with Cedarbrae Eye Doctors to review cataract, glaucoma, retinal, and dry-eye risk in one visit.
